Electric Current

I=ΔqΔt
  • Coulombs/sec = Amperes, or Amp, or A
  • Δq = number of charges moving through area, specifically cross-sectional area of wire

  • If 3 C passes through imaginary plane every sec, then current = 3 Amp

  • Imagine it's positive charge moving ( even though it's usually negative electrons moving )

    • So positive current is defined as the direction of positive charge movement
    • Even though it's really negative electrons moving the opposite direction
    • For example, if negative charges are moving to the left, then positive current is to the right

  • Electrons are pushed along a wire by an electric field inside the wire

    • not static
    • current is flowing

  • They also feel a resistance to movement, kind of like viscosity

  • Long derivation, result is ΔV=I∗R , where:

    • ΔV is voltage difference between 2 places on the wire

    • I is the current

    • R is something called "resistance"

      • R has units of "ohms" =VoltsAmps
      • Symbol Ω

  • Resistance depends on geometry of wire and wire material:
R=b∗Lq2∗n∗A

  • q = charge on moving carrier object (electron)

  • n = density o fcharge carriers

  • b = "viscosity"

    • Depends on material

  • L = length of wire

    • Depends on material

  • A = cross-sectional area of wire

 

  • Important to remember:

    • R∝LA

      • R increases if L increases
      • R increase if A decreases
